"Lost City of the Incas." It was built in the 15th century during the reign of the Inca emperor Pachacuti and was later abandoned during the Spanish conquest.

New Seven Wonders of the World.
Machu Picchu is a breathtaking testament to the engineering brilliance and cultural depth of the Inca civilization. Its mystical beauty and historical significance make it
a must-visit site for travelers and historians alike.



History of Machu Picchu
When and Why Was Machu Picchu Built?

Although local indigenous people knew about Machu Picchu, American historian Hiram Bingham brought it to international attention in 1911. Bingham, exploring for Yale University, was led to the ruins by a local farmer. Machu Picchu was built around 1450 AD during the reign of Inca Emperor Pachacuti. The exact purpose of the site is still debated, but historians believe it may have served as:

A royal estate for the Inca elite
A religious and spiritual center for rituals
An astronomical observatory to track the sun and stars
A military outpost to protect Inca territory

Despite being an advanced civilization, the Incas had no written records, so much of its history remains a mystery.

1. The Sun Gate (Inti Punku)

The Sun Gate (Inti Punku) is one of the most iconic landmarks of Machu Picchu, serving as the main entrance for those hiking the Inca Trail. This site holds historical and cultural significance as it was once a checkpoint for those entering the sacred city.

From the Sun Gate, visitors can enjoy breathtaking panoramic views of Machu Picchu. The site is also famous for its alignment with the sun, particularly during the summer solstice when the sunrise perfectly frames the entrance.

For hikers, reaching the Sun Gate marks the culmination of their journey along the Inca Trail, offering a rewarding first glimpse of the legendary Lost City of the Incas.

2.The Intihuatana Stone

The Intihuatana Stone is a ritual stone associated with astronomy and spiritual practices at Machu Picchu. This carved granite rock is believed to have been used by the Incas as a solar clock or calendar. Its name, meaning "Hitching Post of the Sun," reflects its possible function in tracking celestial events and marking important seasonal changes.

The Intihuatana Stone is one of the most enigmatic and significant structures at Machu Picchu, often referred to as the "Hitching Post of the Sun." It is a carefully carved granite stone believed to have been used as an astronomical and ritual device by the Incas.

The stone was used as a solar clock or calendar, helping Inca priests predict solstices and equinoxes. On the winter solstice (June 21st), the sun aligns almost directly above the stone, casting minimal shadow, signaling the time for important agricultural ceremonies.
